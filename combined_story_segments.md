You consider lying. The calculation takes less than a breath and comes up empty — this woman has spent her life reading terrain, and a face is terrain. You say, in trade-tongue, carefully: 'A colour. Not one that belongs here. Not darkness, not light. Something that exists before the distinction is made.' You watch her face for the reaction most people give — the polite blankness that means they are filing you under eccentric. It does not come. Instead something tightens at the corner of her eye. Recognition, or the cousin of it. 'It has a name in our tongue,' she says, and the bones in her hair click as she looks briefly skyward, as though checking whether the scar might reopen. 'We call it *voreth*. The colour of a thing before it decides what it is. The shamans say the Wellspring bled *voreth* on the day it was first bound — that the Covenant forced it to choose a shape, and it has been grieving the other shapes ever since.' She brings her gaze back down to you. The pale eyes are steady, and for the first time something in them is not purely tactical. 'You are the third person I have met outside the Thornwood who has seen it. The first two were Conclave mages.' A beat. 'They are both dead now. The Wellspring reached back into them through the colour, the shamans say, to reclaim what was taken.' She does not say this as a threat. She says it the way a soldier reports casualties: cleanly, because the truth is less dangerous than the surprise.

You incline your head — a slow, deliberate bow, shallow enough to be courtesy rather than deference, deep enough to be genuine. In Zhennian it means: *I have heard you, and I will not waste what you gave me.* Whether she reads any of that in the angle is uncertain, but Orrath accepts it without comment, which suggests she understands the grammar of restraint. 'Walk carefully,' she says, simply. Not a farewell. A recommendation. You turn and move away through the thinning crowd before she can add anything that might require a response you are not yet ready to give. You find your position against the base of an unbroken column, twenty paces from the ruined arch, angled so that the whole plaza opens before you. Old habit: stone at your back, sightlines clear, two exits within a sprint. The luminous motes drift upward through the failing light, thicker now than minutes ago. A city warden has given up trying to disperse them and simply watches them rise, hands at his sides, in the posture of a man whose training did not cover this. Orrath has not moved toward you. She has returned her attention to the arch — but once, briefly, her eyes find your column with the same calm precision she might use to mark a tree in unfamiliar forest. She knows exactly where you are. The courier boy, Dav Soot, is crouched near the fountain with a heel of bread he produced from nowhere, performing childhood so thoroughly that only the stillness of his ears betrays him; he is listening in a radius of ten feet, cataloguing. Across the plaza, Archmagister Thessaly Vorn has emerged again from the hatch beneath the gate and stands now in the middle of the plaza with a small leather-bound instrument pressed to the cracked capstone. Her mouth is set. Whatever the instrument tells her, she does not like it.

You push off the column and enter the crowd at a walker's pace — not hurrying, because hurry is a flag. The plaza empties into the Coppervane district through a narrow throat of a street called the Stitchway, and you take it, letting the press of evening foot traffic absorb you the way river water absorbs a stone. The city speaks to you as you move. Veranthos is beautiful in the way of things that know they are dying — spired facades still gilded, ley-lanterns humming along the eaves in amber chains, but every third lamp stuttering, and twice in two blocks the cobblestones beneath your feet exhale a faint warmth as though a fire burns just below the surface. A fault-line, buried but present. You mark it. You mark the guard-post at the Stitchway's mouth — two wardens in Throne livery, one attentive, one watching the sky with the hollow expression of a man rationing his fear — and you mark the shrine of the First Archmages at the district crossroads, where someone has left fresh flowers and a broken ward-stone side by side, offering and accusation together. Lamplighter's Arch is easy to find. It stands at the southern end of Coppervane Street proper — a triumphal arch from some older war, the stone carved with flame-motifs now blackened by the ley-lanterns hung beneath it in dense clusters, a dozen extinguished among them. From the arch you can count: two approach routes from the north, one alley to the east narrow enough to make pursuit ugly, a cooper's workshop across the street with a hayloft window overlooking the meeting point at roughly forty degrees elevation. That window could be a problem. The arch itself has a deep alcove on its western face — shadow even at noon, a natural place to put a sword or a conversation you want no one to overhear. Someone chose this location with care. You spend eight minutes on the scout, moving in a wide spiral, cataloguing. You find no watchers you can name. That is not the same as finding none.

You have two hours before second bell, and an empty hand changes every calculation you made at Lamplighter's Arch. Coppervane is a merchant district — there should be ironwork within three streets in any direction. You turn east off the main road, following the smell of coal-smoke and the particular clang of a hammer finding a cold join rather than a hot one: a smith winding down for the evening, not starting up. The first forge you find is shuttered, chain across the door, a notice in Avelornian script you can read only poorly. The second is open but the smith — a broad woman with red forearms and no patience — takes one look at your foreign face and the empty purse you produce and turns back to her cooling rack without a word. The third is not a smith at all but a tinker's cart wedged in an alley mouth, the tinker himself already loading unsold stock into canvas bags with the focused economy of a man who lost money today and intends to lose no more time. You scan the cart. Pot-menders, lamp-hooks, a broken pruning blade, small agricultural tools. Nothing you would trust your life to. You try three words of trade-tongue — knife, sell, price — and the tinker looks at your empty hands and empty purse and the foreign cut of your coat and makes the same calculation the smith made. He shakes his head before you finish the sentence. The light is going amber at the rooftop level. You have walked in a narrowing circle and come back to Coppervane Street with nothing changed except the hour, which has changed against you. The arch is visible from here, its extinguished lanterns dark against the dusk. Second bell is closer than you would like. You are exactly as empty-handed as when you started.

You find the corner without searching for it — a recess between a chandler's shuttered frontage and the blank stone shoulder of the arch itself, deep enough to swallow you, angled so that the western alcove and the hayloft window both sit within your peripheral field. The laugh you allow yourself is a quiet thing, barely breath. You have attended meetings with nothing before. You once walked into the exam of the Seventh Form with a broken wrist and emerged with the highest mark of your cohort. The empty hands are familiar. They are, in a sense, the only honest introduction you have left to make. You fold your legs beneath you, spine long, and let the city's noise become texture rather than information. The warm fault-line pulse beneath the cobblestones rises through your sitting bones in a slow, irregular rhythm — not a heartbeat, something older and less certain than a heartbeat. The luminous motes you first saw at the Auroch Gate are here too, sparse but present, drifting at knee-height through the dusk like embers from a fire you cannot locate. One passes through the edge of your hand. You feel nothing. Or almost nothing. A faint resonance, low and nameless, in the centre of your sternum — the place where the Crane-in-Storm form tells you to anchor your breath. You note it. You do not pursue it. The second bell begins to ring somewhere deeper in the city. It is a cracked sound — the same bell you heard on arrival, still carrying its wound. In the western alcove of Lamplighter's Arch, the shadows shift. Someone has been there for some time. They waited for the bell before they moved, and that tells you something about how they prefer the arithmetic of meetings: let the other person wonder. You open your eyes fully and rise in a single unhurried motion, the way water levels itself. Empty hands at your sides. You cross the six paces to the arch in silence.

The alcove gives her up slowly. She is not tall — you register that first, because the note had the authority of height and she does not have it. What she has instead is stillness, the particular stillness of someone who has learned to make a room forget they are in it. She is perhaps fifty, perhaps older, Avelornian by her cheekbones and the silver threadwork at her collar, which is Conclave-adjacent but not Conclave — the weave is close, the colour wrong, a deep grey where the Arcane College wears blue. Her hair is white and pinned without vanity. Her hands rest at her sides, open, visible. She made sure you would see that. The bisected-eye mark is embroidered, small and precise, on her left cuff. She holds the silence for three full breaths after you stop moving. Letting you look. Letting you measure. When she speaks, it is in trade-tongue, but her Zhennian is there underneath the words like bedrock under soil: 'You did not burn the note.' She does not sound disappointed. She sounds like she knew. 'My name is Serevaine. I have no title I currently acknowledge. I knew your teacher before he was your teacher, which is a longer story than we have standing in an archway.' She takes one step forward into the last of the dusk light — deliberate, un-aggressive, offering her face to your read. 'I need to know one thing before I tell you anything useful. The mote. Near the second column from the fountain, during your meditation. What did you feel when it passed through you?' Her voice does not change register asking it. But her hands, still open at her sides, have gone very still.

You let the silence run one breath longer than is comfortable — not to be difficult, but because the words for this do not exist in trade-tongue and you are building them from what you have. When you speak, your voice is low and unhurried, the same register you use to explain a technique to a student who has not yet earned the full explanation. 'A single note. Below sound — felt rather than heard. It lived here' — your right hand lifts and presses two fingers flat against your sternum, between the third and fourth rib, exactly where the Crane-in-Storm form places the root of the breath-column — 'and it did not arrive. It was already there. The mote passed through and it recognised something. Or something in me recognised the mote. I cannot tell you which.' You lower your hand. 'It did not feel like intrusion. It felt like a door opening that I did not know was a door. And it closed again without asking my preference.' You hold her gaze steadily. 'I have felt that precise location once before. The morning my teacher handed me something I was not permitted to carry out of Zhennian, and told me I would be exiled before nightfall. He pressed two fingers here, the same way, and said nothing. I did not understand it then.' You stop. You have told her exactly what she asked and not one word beyond it. The space after the words is hers now. Her face has done something — not surprise, which would be dishonest, but a kind of careful settling, the way a navigator who has been estimating by the stars suddenly gets a fixed landmark. Her open hands at her sides have not moved. But her composure has shifted by some small, legible degree inward, toward something that in another person might be relief.

The words land softly, but they land like a weight placed on a scale. *You said you knew him.* Not a question. Not an accusation. A boundary, drawn with a single sentence, precisely where she pressed. Serevaine's composure does not crack. But something behind it shifts — she was expecting deflection, or demand, or fear. She was not expecting the counter-offered silence of someone who has decided the next move is hers to earn. Her pale eyes move once to your hands, which have not reached for anything because there is nothing to reach for, but which have settled differently at your sides — weight forward, not back, the Crane-in-Storm root planted in the cobblestone through your soles. She reads it accurately. She does not step back. To her credit. 'I knew him as Liang Sorveth,' she says. 'Which is not the name he gave you, I think, and not the name the Zhennian court has on any record.' She watches the name land. 'He came to Avelorn seventeen years ago carrying a fragment of something he should not have been able to carry — something that should have been sealed here, beneath the Auric Mountains, for another thousand years. He said he had borrowed it.' The faint emphasis on *borrowed* implies a history with the word. 'He went back east. He marked a student and exiled him toward a cracking world and a meeting at second bell, which means he knew both were coming.' She tilts her head, the smallest increment. 'I am not your enemy tonight, Ming. But I am asking: what did he give you to carry? Not what you think it is. What it actually is — because your body already knows, even if your mind has not caught it yet.'

You hold the preparation a moment longer — the weight-forward stance, the open calculation — and then you release it. Not because the threat has passed. Because she has already told you more than she meant to and the fastest way to the rest is to give her something real and watch where it lands. 'I carried nothing I could name,' you say. 'Nothing with shape or weight. When my teacher pressed his fingers here' — the same two-finger gesture against the sternum, a habit already — 'I felt a warmth that stayed. For seventeen days after, I dreamed in a colour I had no word for. Then it faded, and I decided I had imagined it. I decided the exile itself was the punishment, and the warmth was grief, and the colour was what grief looks like from the inside.' A pause, thin as a blade. 'Three hours ago I walked through the Auroch Gate and the sky folded and I saw the colour again. Not grief. Not imagined. And when the mote touched me in meditation, it found the warmth still there — not faded. Waiting.' You watch her face with the specific attention you once used to read the angle of a spear before it moved. 'He did not give me an object. He gave me a location. He built a room inside my body and put something in it and bricked the door and sent me to the one city in the world where the wall between rooms is breaking.' The words cost something to say aloud. The shame behind the sternum shifts — not smaller, but different in shape, because exile-as-engineering is a different wound than exile-as-failure. 'I do not know what I am carrying. But I think you do. And I think your face just confirmed it.' Serevaine's composure does not shatter. But it moves. Her open hands rise — not defensively, not threateningly — and she presses her own fingertips briefly to her sternum, an involuntary mirror of your gesture, and then catches herself doing it and lowers them with the deliberate care of someone reclaiming a tell. The bisected-eye mark on her cuff catches the last dusk-light. When she speaks, her voice is half a degree quieter than it has been, which on a woman of this stillness is the equivalent of shouting. 'He called it a Shard of Prior Covenant,' she says. 'Not a fragment of the Wellspring. Older than that. A piece of what the Wellspring was bound from.' Her eyes hold yours. 'The Sealers believe they need four cardinal shrine-components to re-bind the Wellspring. They are wrong. They need five. The fifth is not at a shrine.' She lets the silence complete the sentence. 'Your teacher was supposed to return it seventeen years ago. He did not. He chose you instead, which means he believed you would reach this city at this exact moment and that the Sundering Clock would have forty-seven days remaining when you did.' A breath. 'He also believed you would be difficult to convince. He asked me to tell you one thing, if all else failed.' Her composure is fully back now, settled like armour. 'He said to tell you: the door was always yours. He only built the frame.'

The words come out clean and level, the way truth does when it has been carried a long time and finally set down. *I was banished because I won the fight I shouldn't win.* No bitterness in it. No performance. Just the stone behind the sternum, named aloud for the first time in a foreign city to a foreign woman who claims to have been waiting seventeen years for exactly this moment. Serevaine is quiet for longer than she has been quiet before. The dusk has finished its work — the arch lanterns that still function have come on around you, amber and guttering, and in their light her face is readable in a way that full daylight might have concealed. She is not calculating. She is, for the first time, simply listening. When she speaks, her voice carries no persuasion in it. That absence is itself a kind of respect. 'I know,' she says. 'He told me. A prodigy who broke the form that was not supposed to be broken, beat the master who was not supposed to be beatable, and was removed from the board before anyone had to decide what he meant.' A pause. 'The Zhennian court exiled you. Liang Sorveth handed you a burning brand to carry across a continent and pointed you at a cracking world. Those are also fights you did not choose.' She lets that sit. 'But Ming.' Her voice drops further, not softer — more precise, the way a blade gets when it is being used for its actual purpose. 'The Shard in your sternum does not care what you were banished for. The Wellspring does not care whether you came here willingly. The forty-seven days will expire regardless of your concern.' She tilts her head, and something almost human crosses her face — not pity, but acknowledgment, the recognition of one exiled thing naming another. 'You said you have nothing to lose. I am telling you that is not true anymore. You walked through the Auroch Gate carrying the only piece that can prevent a rupture that will unmake every kingdom, every exile, and every fight you ever won or lost. The door was always yours.' She exhales slowly. 'The question is not whether this is your concern. The question is what you want to do with a concern you were given without your consent — and whether the man who gave it to you deserves an answer.'

The question cuts through everything she has built — the careful framing, the patience, the teacher's last words deployed like a key. You ask it the way you would ask the range of a weapon before deciding whether to close distance: not hostility, not warmth. Arithmetic. *What do you want from me. What do you get.* Serevaine does not look away. She does not reach for composure because she has not lost it, but something behind her eyes makes a decision, and the decision is honesty, and honesty costs her something visible. 'I want the Shard delivered intact to the fifth-point of the re-binding ritual beneath the Auric Mountains before the Sundering Clock expires,' she says. 'That is the simple part.' She takes one slow breath. 'What I get — what any of us get — is a world that continues to exist in a form recognisable to the people currently living in it. That is not a small thing, but I understand it is also not yours.' Her hands come together at her waist, not clasped — fingers resting against fingers, the gesture of a person holding themselves to precision. 'I should tell you the rest.' The quality of the silence shifts. 'The Sealers do not know a fifth component exists. Archmagister Vorn is a careful woman and a genuine believer, but she is working from an incomplete Covenant-text, and if she attempts the re-binding without the Prior Shard she will fail — the rupture will accelerate rather than seal, and the failure will consume everyone in the ritual chamber.' A pause that has weight in it. 'I have been trying to tell her for three years. She believes I am a Quietist agent. I am not a Quietist agent. I am something older and considerably more inconvenient.' The bisected-eye mark on her cuff catches the amber light. 'As for what the ritual requires of you specifically — the Shard cannot be removed from a living vessel by force. It can only be offered. Willingly. At the site.' She holds your gaze with the steady attention of a woman who has rehearsed this sentence and hates every word of it. 'The texts are ambiguous on whether the vessel survives the offering. I will not lie to you about that. Liang Sorveth did not ask your consent. I am asking it. Those are different things, and I know they are not the same as a guarantee.' She stops. The cracked bell in the city's interior has long since finished its count. The alcove holds the two of you and the amber guttering and the fault-line warmth rising through the stones beneath your feet, patient as an old wound.

The question arrives with the precision of a spear-tip finding the gap between plates. Not the fact of possible death — you have lived adjacent to that since the exile — but the architecture of the ambiguity. Whether the texts are vague about how the offering works, or vague about what it does to the body that offers. Those are different uncertainties. You ask both. And then, because the bisected-eye mark has been sitting in your peripheral attention since she stepped into the light: who wrote them. Serevaine is still for a moment that has a different quality than her previous stillnesses. This one is not composure over calculation. It is composure over something that resembles old grief, and you file that away immediately. 'The method is precise,' she says. 'The Shard must be at the fifth-point of the ritual geometry. The vessel must speak the release — a single phrase in Prior Covenant tongue, which I can teach you. The Wellspring does the rest. There is no knife involved. No extraction. You stand at the centre of something older than the kingdom and you open the door.' Her fingers press flat against her own sternum before she catches them and lowers them, and this time she does not pretend she did not do it. She has stopped pretending. 'The ambiguity is entirely in the outcome. Three texts survive that describe the Prior Covenant. Two were written by the First Archmages themselves — who sealed the Wellspring originally, and who were not vessels. They were theorising. The third text is fragmentary and in a script we spent eleven years partially translating.' A pause with an edge to it. 'That text suggests the vessel and the Shard are not separable in the way the Archmage texts assume. That seventeen years of carrying may have created a correspondence — that the Shard has become partly Ming-shaped, and Ming has become partly Shard-shaped, and what happens when that geometry is released at the fifth-point is genuinely unknown.' She meets your eyes and does not soften it. 'The Archmage texts say the vessel is emptied and walks away. The third text says the door and the frame cannot be taken apart without consequence to both. I do not know which is correct. I have spent three years trying to find out and I have forty-four days left and you are the only person who can answer the question from the inside.' The amber lantern above the alcove gutters in a draft carrying the faint copper-salt smell of a Fault-scar somewhere upwind. 'As for who wrote the third text.' She reaches into the grey coat and produces something small — a folded paper, old, the creases worn soft — and holds it out without releasing it yet. 'It was found seventeen years ago in the personal effects of a Far-Eastern scholar who came to Avelorn and left again without permission. The Conclave confiscated it. I borrowed it from the Conclave.' The word borrowed carries the same weight it did when she used it of Liang Sorveth, and she knows you hear that. 'Your teacher wrote the third text, Ming. He wrote it here, in this city, before he went back east and built a frame around a door and sent it home inside a student.' She extends the folded paper the remaining distance. 'It is written in your language. I cannot read it. I have been carrying it for seventeen years waiting for someone who could.'

You take the paper from her fingers without hesitation. That itself is information you give her freely — that you are not afraid of what your teacher wrote, or that you are afraid and have decided it does not matter which. The paper is old in the specific way of things that have been handled often: the folds soft as cloth, the edges worn to a fine chamois smoothness. Someone has held this many times without opening it. You open it. The script is your home language — not the formal court-hand you were taught in the academies, but the personal register your teacher used when he corrected your forms with margin-notes. The characters are smaller than they should be, pressed close, written in the cramped urgency of someone who had very little time and a great deal to say. You read it twice. What Liang Sorveth wrote in this city seventeen years ago is this: the Shard is not a piece of the Wellspring's binding. It is a piece of the Wellspring's memory — a fragment of a will that chose to become the Wellspring, the part that refused to be sealed, which could not be contained in stone and could only survive inside a living body that was itself in exile from where it belonged. He writes that he carried it for eleven years and it changed him in ways he did not consent to and could not undo. He writes that he chose Ming not because Ming was the best student, but because Ming already knew what it was to be something that had refused to stay inside the shape the world assigned it. And then, pressed into the bottom margin in smaller characters, added at the last possible moment: do not let them use you as a key. You are not a key. Find out what the Wellspring wills before you decide whether to offer anything at all. You lower the paper. You do not look up immediately. You give yourself three full seconds and in those three seconds you are aware that Serevaine has not moved, has not spoken, and has been watching your face with the attention of someone who has waited seventeen years for this particular expression. You look up. Your face has done one thing it does not do: it has gone completely still in the way that is not composure. It is the stillness of a man who has just learned that the worst thing that ever happened to him was also a gift, and that both facts are equally true, and that he does not yet know what to do with either.

Tanners' Row is exactly what its name promises — low buildings, the ghost of cured hide in the cold air, lanterns spaced far enough apart that the dark between them is full and private. The Undyed Cloth is a narrow place, three storeys, its sign a pale rectangle of unfinished wool. The woman Passa opens the door before you knock, which means Serevaine sent word by a route faster than your walking, and you file that information away in the part of the mind that does not sleep. Passa is broad, grey-haired, and entirely incurious — the particular incuriosity of a professional, which is its own kind of trustworthiness. She hands you a key and points at the stairs without speaking. You are grateful for this more than you expected to be. The room is small and clean. A basin, a pitcher of water still faintly warm, a bed with a quilt the colour of undyed wool, a single candle. You eat the bread and hard cheese left on the table beside the basin and you do not think about the paper or the sternum or the forty-seven days. You eat because the body that carries the Shard is still a body, and it walked a long way today through a folded sky, and it is owed bread and cheese and the absence of consequence for a few hours. The food is plain and adequate and you finish all of it. You sleep. And in the sleep — not immediately, not in the shallow water near the surface, but deep in, where discipline does not reach — the colour comes again. Voreth. Not a dream exactly. More like the inside of the door, lit from the other side. It does not speak. It does not reach. It is simply present the way the warmth in your sternum is present: already there, patient, waiting to find out what you will decide to make of it. You sleep through it without waking. That itself is something. The old Ming would have woken. The exile carrying a splinter of a god's refusal sleeps through the colour of a thing before it decides what it is, and in the morning the candle has burned to nothing and the light coming through the shutter-crack is the pale grey of an hour before the city remembers it is a city.

You wash your face in the basin's cold water, dress, and fold Liang Sorveth's paper back into your inner pocket with the deliberate care of a man filing a weapon he is not yet ready to use. Then you go downstairs. Passa is already in the narrow kitchen, moving between hearth and table with the economy of someone who has opened this house every morning for a very long time. She sets a bowl of porridge in front of you without being asked and pours something hot and dark that is not tea but functions as tea, and you eat half of it before you ask. 'Seventeen years ago,' you say, in the careful Avelornian you have been building since the border. 'A Far-Eastern scholar. He would have stayed somewhere like this. He would have been quiet and inconvenient in equal measure.' You pause. 'He went by at least two names.' Passa refills her own cup and sits across from you, which she did not do last night, and looks at you with the particular attention of a woman deciding how much something is worth. 'Seventeen years is a long time,' she says. 'I have been running this house for twenty-three.' She wraps both hands around her cup. 'There was a man. Eastern eyes, like yours, but older — maybe sixty when I knew him, maybe more. He stayed four months, which is unusual. Paid in advance, which is less unusual. Wrote constantly, all hours. The ink stains on the desk in room four are his — I stopped trying to sand them out.' A pause. 'He left one morning without warning, which they do. But he left something behind. Not forgot — left deliberately, propped against the pillow like a decision.' She gets up, opens the small locked box on the shelf above the hearth that you clocked last night and filed away as her business, and sets a small flat object on the table between the bowls. It is a carved token — dark wood, the size of a thumb — incised with a single character in your home script. Not a word. A name-mark. His. 'I kept it because it felt wrong to throw it away,' Passa says. 'I kept it because seventeen years ago a woman with a bisected-eye mark on her cuff came by asking about him the week after he left, and she looked at it and said: hold onto that. Someone will come for it eventually.' She meets your eyes across the table with perfect uncuriosity. 'I imagine that is you.'

You pick up the name-token and hold it without closing your fist around it — the same way you hold a weapon you are not yet sure is yours. The carved character is his, unmistakably: the particular stroke-weight he used when signing corrections, the small hook at the tail that was an idiosyncrasy of his province. You set it beside the bowl and look at Passa. 'What was he like,' you say. 'In the four months. His habits. Whether he had visitors. Whether he seemed afraid.' Passa drinks from her cup and considers this with the same unhurried attention she has given everything so far — the attention of a woman who has housed a great many people and learned that the ones worth remembering are rarely the ones who announce themselves. 'Disciplined,' she says first. 'Up before the house, asleep after it. He ate what was given and never complained and never left food unfinished, which I appreciate. He moved quietly — not like a man hiding, more like a man who had decided noise was a waste.' She pauses. 'He meditated in the courtyard each morning. Not praying — something more physical. Breathing work, and a stillness that was not stillness. His hands moved sometimes, very slightly, as if he was practising something too small to see.' She glances at your hands around the bowl without making a point of it. 'He had one visitor. Twice. A young Avelornian — Conclave robes but worn like he was embarrassed by them, maybe thirty years old at the time. They spoke in the courtyard in low voices and the second time the visitor left quickly and did not come back.' 'The fear,' she says, and pauses again, deciding something. 'Not afraid of people. He watched the sky — the direction of the Auric Mountains specifically, every evening, the way a sailor watches weather. Three weeks before he left, he stopped sleeping through the night. I heard him moving in room four. I heard the pen. He was writing faster at the end than at the beginning.' She wraps both hands around her cup again. 'The morning he left he came down early and ate everything on the table — everything, mine included, which he had never done — and he looked at me the way people look at a place they know they will not see again. He said: you will know who to give it to because they will not ask what it is worth.' She meets your eyes. 'You did not ask.'

Passa looks at you across the table with the expression of a woman who has already decided the answer and is simply waiting for the question to finish arriving. She stands without ceremony, picks up a candle from the shelf — the morning light doesn't reach the upper floors yet — and tilts her head toward the stairs. She does not ask why. Room four is the second door on the left. It is the same dimensions as yours, the same quilt, the same shutter-crack letting in the grey early light. But the desk is different. It is an old piece, the wood dark with age, and the surface has been sanded and resanded over many years in the way of something a careful person keeps trying to make clean. The stains remain. Not a spill — not the careless pooling of an overturned inkstone. These are the stains of sustained, pressured writing: the fine spray that comes from a brush moved fast, the heavier blot where a hand stopped mid-stroke and held, the long thin trail of a character begun and abandoned. You recognise the pattern of the abandoned character. It is the same one on the name-token in your pocket. He was practising writing his own name and stopped. You stand at the desk and look at it for a moment that is longer than it should be. Passa stands in the doorway with the candle, watching you with the patient attention she has given everything this morning. She does not fill the silence. The stains are seventeen years old and they still smell, very faintly, of ink and something else — the mineral-cold scent that clings to a place near a Fault-scar. Faint, old, almost not there. But you know it now. Your sternum knows it. He was working here when the Wellspring began to bleed, or perhaps he was working here and the Wellspring began to bleed in response, and you do not yet know which direction the causation runs.

You press two fingers to the desk. Not the sternum gesture — a different thing, deliberate: fingertips flat against the oldest ink-stain, the heaviest one, where he held the brush too long and let the ink pool. The wood is cold and smooth and for a moment there is nothing except the faint mineral smell and the grey light through the shutter-crack and Passa breathing quietly in the doorway. Then: not warmth. Not the clean resonance you felt in the Fault-scar's folded sky. Something partial. Something that comes in sideways, like a note played in an adjacent room, heard through stone. The Shard stirs — the door behind your sternum shifts a fraction on its hinge — and for two or three heartbeats you are aware of a presence that is not yours and is not the room's. It is the trace of his presence. Not him. Not his will. The residue of a man who sat here for four months carrying what you now carry, writing faster as the Wellspring began to bleed beneath the mountains, feeling the same door shift in his own chest. The correspondence is real and it is old and it ends before it becomes anything you can hold. The Shard subsides. The wood is cold again. What you are left with is not knowledge — it is the specific texture of recognition, the feeling of a lock that fits a key you have been carrying without knowing it was a key, and your teacher's warning rises in the back of your throat like bile: you are not a key. You stand at the desk with your fingers still flat against the stain and your composure entirely intact because composure is what you have instead of the thing you are not going to feel right now, and behind you Passa says, very quietly: 'Your hands are shaking.'

You take your fingers off the desk. You do not look at Passa. You walk past her through the doorway, down the stairs, through the narrow kitchen still smelling of porridge and dark tea, and out the front door of the Undyed Cloth into Tanners' Row. The cold air of early morning hits your face and you stand in it and breathe through your nose, four counts, hold, eight counts out — the discipline your teacher taught you when you were eleven years old and did not yet know that everything he gave you was also a frame built around a door he intended to send away. The irony of using his breathing to steady yourself against the knowledge of what he did lands somewhere beneath your composure and you let it land and you do not move. The street is empty except for a carter at the far end unloading hides in the half-dark, moving with the slow economy of a man who has done this ten thousand mornings. The Fault-scar warmth rises through the cobblestones. The Shard sits behind your sternum, quiet now, the way it is always quiet after it has said something. Your hands have stopped shaking. You made them stop on the stairs. The name-token is in your pocket and the third text is in your inner coat and your teacher built a frame around a door and sent it home in a student and you are standing on a street in a foreign city with forty-four days left and you do not yet know what the Wellspring wills. You know what everyone else wills. You breathe. The city remembers it is a city: a shutter opens above you, somewhere a bell counts the hour, the carter's horse shifts its weight on the cobblestones. You are still here. That is enough, for this exact moment. That is sufficient.

You walk. Not toward anything. Tanners' Row gives onto a broader street of dye-merchants still shuttered, which gives onto a market square beginning its slow morning assembly — vendors unrolling canvas, a boy carrying a stack of crates taller than his head, two city guards with the particular vacancy of men ending a long night. You move through it all at the pace of someone who belongs, which is the pace you default to when you need your mind free of itself. The spear is not on your back — too conspicuous for a city this size — but the muscle-memory of its weight is still there, and you let your shoulders carry the phantom of it while you walk. The city helps, which you did not expect. It is too large and too indifferent to care about a door behind a Far-Eastern exile's sternum. A woman is arguing with a fish-seller in the rapid idiomatic Avelornian you are still mapping. A Conclave courier cuts through the square at a near-run with sealed documents under his arm and does not look at you. The Fault-scar warmth is lower here, fainter, just below perception — the city's normal floor. You walk for long enough that the sun clears the eastern spires and puts proper gold on the cobblestones, and somewhere in the third or fourth street after the market your mind stops pulling at the same two facts and starts laying them beside each other instead. Your teacher carried the Shard for eleven years and it changed him without his consent. He chose you because you already knew what it was to refuse the shape the world assigned you. He warned you not to be a key. All of this is true. Also true: he built the frame without asking you. He made the decision that changed the entire architecture of your life — the exile, the years alone, the discipline that became composure because you had no one left to be soft with — and he made it in a room in this city while a Sealer watched, and he sent you west carrying something you did not know was there and called it your door. You stop at a low bridge over one of the city's inner canals and put your hands on the stone railing and look at the water below. He was not wrong about you. That is the part that makes the anger complicated. He read you correctly and used what he read, and those two things are not the same as love even if they were not the opposite of it either. The water moves. The Shard is quiet. You have forty-four days and a name-token in your pocket and a woman with a bisected eye who has been waiting three years for Vorn to listen. The shock is not gone. It has simply found its level, the way water does, and below the level is something that might eventually be usable. You are still here. You know what Liang Sorveth did. You do not yet know what you will do with knowing it. That is enough. That is the next honest step.
